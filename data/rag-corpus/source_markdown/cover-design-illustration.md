## Service Tiers

Cover design pricing has two dimensions: **layout** (how much surface area we're designing) and **complexity** (how intricate the design is). Per-design rates apply across both.

### Cover layouts

| Layout | Best for | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Front Only** | eBook-only releases, retailer thumbnails | Single front face — optimized for digital storefronts and small-screen browsing |
| **Front + Back** | Paperback and hardcover | Front cover, back cover (with blurb area), and spine — required for any print edition |
| **Full Wrap** | Premium hardcover, illustrated editions | Continuous wraparound design flowing across front, spine, and back — premium presentation |

| Complexity | Per-design rate (Front Only / Front+Back / Full Wrap) |

| **Premium** — custom illustration, intricate detail, hand-lettered typography, multiple revision rounds included |

## What Influences Cost & Timeline

Beyond the layout and complexity tiers above, these factors shift price and time:

- **Illustration style** — Photographic compositing is faster than custom illustration; full hand-painted artwork takes the longest
- **Custom typography** — Off-the-shelf fonts are included; custom lettering or commissioned typography is an add-on
- **Spine design** — Required for print covers; minimal effort for thin books, more design work for thick books with title-on-spine layout
- **Number of elements** — Single-character covers are simpler than ensemble compositions
- **Background complexity** — Solid color backgrounds are simpler than detailed environments or scenes
- **Symbolism/thematic elements** — Covers conveying multiple thematic layers via embedded symbols take more concept work

## Our Design Process

1. **Initial consultation & briefing.** We gather your book's details — genre, themes, target audience, tone, and any visual references or "books I love the cover of" examples. For first-time authors, we walk through the design process in detail; for returning authors, briefing is faster.

2. **Concept development.** The design team creates 1–3 distinct preliminary concepts. These may include rough sketches, mood boards, or comps — we explore different directions before committing to one. For illustrated covers, we decide whether custom illustration is needed or if photographic/typographic approaches will serve the book better.

3. **Concept presentation & feedback.** We present the concepts with rationale for each. You provide feedback — what works, what doesn't, what to combine. We document the chosen direction and any revision requests in writing so nothing gets lost.

4. **Design refinement & illustration.** The selected concept becomes the full cover. If illustration is involved, the artist commissions and integrates the artwork. Typography is finalized — title, subtitle, author name, and tagline if applicable.

5. **Revision rounds.** You see the refined design and request changes. Two to three revision rounds are typically included in your tier — additional rounds are available as add-ons. We track every change so we know which version is final.

6. **Final approval & delivery.** Once you approve, we prepare delivery files in every format you need: print-ready PDF (with bleed and trim marks for paperback/hardcover), high-res JPEG/PNG for eBook retailers, and audiobook square format if needed. Color profiles are correct for each platform (CMYK for print, sRGB for digital).

## Technical Specifications

- **Print covers:** PDF with 0.125" bleed on all sides, CMYK color profile, 300 DPI minimum, fonts embedded or outlined. Spine width calculated from page count and paper stock.
- **eBook covers:** JPEG or PNG, RGB color profile, minimum 1600×2400 pixels (2:3 aspect ratio), 72–300 DPI. Amazon KDP recommends 2560×1600 for best display.
- **Audiobook covers:** JPEG, square 2400×2400 pixels minimum, RGB, 72 DPI. Required for ACX, Apple Books Audio, Spotify Audiobooks. (Available as Audiobook Cover Adaptation add-on.)
- **Print specifications:** We design to KDP, IngramSpark, and Lulu specifications by default. Other print partners (Mixam, BookBaby, BookVault) supported on request.
- **Source files:** Adobe Photoshop (.psd), Illustrator (.ai), or InDesign (.indd) source files are available on request — useful if you want to make minor edits in-house later.

## Common Questions

**What is included in your cover design service?**
Our cover design service includes a complete pipeline: discovery briefing, concept development with 1–3 preliminary directions, presentation and feedback, full design refinement, the revision rounds included in your tier, and final delivery in print-ready and digital formats. For illustrated covers, custom illustration is part of the package.

**Can I see samples of previous covers you've designed?**
Yes. We can provide a portfolio of anonymized covers from past projects matched to your genre, so you can see our range of style and quality. Some clients prefer to keep their covers private (especially for ghostwritten projects); the portfolio respects those NDAs.

**How do you ensure the cover reflects the book's genre?**
Genre signaling is critical for retailer-page conversion. We research current genre conventions before designing — what fonts, colors, layouts, and image types are converting well in your category right now. We then design within those conventions while finding distinctive elements that make your book stand out from comp titles. The result reads as "right shelf, but not generic."

**Can I request revisions to the cover design?**
Yes. Two to three revision rounds are included in your tier. Additional rounds are as an add-on. We've found that the structured "concept → refinement → revisions" process produces better outcomes than unlimited iterations — clear feedback at each stage gets to the right cover faster.

**What formats will the final cover design be delivered in?**
You receive every format you need: print-ready PDF (with bleed and trim marks) for paperback and hardcover, high-res JPEG and PNG for eBook retailers, and a 2400×2400 square JPEG for audiobook platforms (if you've included that adaptation). Source files (PSD, AI, or INDD) are available on request.

**How long does the cover design process typically take?**

Timeline is calculated by our central Pricing & Timeline engine based on your project parameters. Ask me for an estimate and I'll return a precise range.

**Can you design covers for self-published books on Amazon KDP?**
Yes — KDP is the most common destination for our covers. We design to KDP's exact specifications: 2560×1600 minimum for eBook, print PDF with proper bleed and spine width, and a separate audiobook cover if you're publishing the audio edition through ACX. We can submit the files directly through your KDP account if helpful.

**Can you create alternative cover designs for promotional purposes?**
Yes. Common alternates include season-themed variants for promotional pushes, "limited edition" covers with foil or special finishes, and territory-specific variants (US vs UK markets often respond to different design conventions). Each alternate is priced as a new cover at your tier rate.

**Can you create illustrations for chapter headings or section dividers?**
Yes — Chapter Header Illustrations are available as a per-piece add-on. For books with consistent chapter ornamentation, we can also create a Series Style Guide so book 2 onward inherits the same visual system.

**Can you create covers with embossed or foil elements?**
Yes. Print finishes like foil stamping, embossing, deboss, spot UV, and special laminations all require properly-prepped spec files in addition to the cover artwork. The Foil Stamping/Embossing Design and Special Finishes Design add-ons cover this prep. The actual application is a print-shop process — we coordinate with your print partner to ensure specs match their press capabilities.

**How do you handle covers for multi-genre books?**
Multi-genre books (e.g., a thriller with strong romance elements, or a memoir that's also a business book) require a primary-genre decision. We help you identify which genre's readers you most need to convert, then design to that primary genre's conventions while signaling secondary-genre cues through subtler design choices (typography, secondary imagery). This is a briefing-stage conversation.
